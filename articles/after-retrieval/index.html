On AI, Knowledge & Learning

After Retrieval

What schools and jobs become when knowledge is inferable

7 June 2026

AILLMsKnowledgeEducationEpistemologyPhilosophy

Every revolution in human history has been a revolution in knowledge. Not in what we know — but in how we store it, transmit it, and decide who gets to access it.

The arrival of generative AI is not a software story. It is the next chapter in a 40,000-year-old project: the externalization of human memory. To see why this matters — and where it might lead — we need to look backwards.

🧠 What is knowledge, actually?

Before we trace the history, we need a working definition. Plato gave us the most enduring answer in Theaetetus: knowledge is justified true belief. To know something, you need to believe it, it needs to be true, and you need a reason for believing it. Aristotle then split knowledge into three modes that still structure how we think about expertise:

  • Episteme — universal, theoretical knowledge (what we'd call science)
  • Techne — practical, craft knowledge (what we'd call engineering)
  • Phronesis — practical wisdom (what we'd call judgment)

In the 20th century, the chemist-philosopher Michael Polanyi added another distinction: tacit vs. explicit knowledge. Some knowledge can be written down (facts, procedures, definitions). Some lives only in bodies and habits (the surgeon's hand, the chef's instinct, the trader's gut). “We can know more than we can tell,” Polanyi wrote. This matters now more than ever, because AI is unusually good at codifying the explicit and unusually weak at the tacit.

Knowledge management theorists later gave us the DIKW pyramid: Data → Information → Knowledge → Wisdom. Data is raw signal. Information is data with context. Knowledge is information that has been internalized and connected. Wisdom is knowledge applied with judgment.

Hold these distinctions. The history of knowledge revolutions is largely a history of compressing and distributing the lower layers.

🗿 The substrate problem

Every form of knowledge has been bottlenecked by its medium.

Oral knowledge dies with the speaker. Written knowledge is fragile to fire. Printed knowledge is expensive to copy until you have a press. Digital knowledge is cheap to copy but expensive to verify. Modeled knowledge, what a large language model contains, is cheap to query but opaque to interrogate.

Every revolution that follows is a story about substrate. About finding a new way to store more, share more, or compute more on what humanity already knows.

📜 The long arc

Eighteen moments. Each one a new way to store, transmit, or compute what we know. Read top to bottom — the further you descend, the closer we get to now.

~40,000 BCE

Cave paintings & symbolic markers

First external memories outside the human body.

to 3000 BCE

Oral traditions

Knowledge synchronous, embodied, fading with each keeper.

~3400 BCE

Invention of writing

Cuneiform and hieroglyphs. Knowledge becomes durable.

~1800 BCE

Mathematical notation

Abstract symbols that compress reasoning itself.

~1050 BCE

Phoenician alphabet

22 signs. Literacy becomes learnable in a day.

c. 300 BCE

The great libraries

Alexandria, Nalanda, Baghdad. Knowledge aggregated and translated.

c. 100 BCE

Paper & codex

The first searchable medium. Page replaces scroll.

1440

The printing press

Reproducing text becomes cheap. Ideas outrun their censors.

1665

Scientific journals

Peer-reviewed, dated, citation-traceable knowledge.

17th–18th c.

Newspapers & postal networks

Information becomes time-stamped and routine.

1830s–1920s

The telegraph, telephone & radio

Signal detaches from physical transport. Voice returns at broadcast scale.

1890s

Cinema & television

Industrial moving images at mass scale.

1940s

Computers

Knowledge stored as voltage states. The substrate goes electronic.

1989

The World Wide Web

Documents link to documents. The library is everywhere.

1998–2001

Search engines & Wikipedia

Algorithmic ranking. The encyclopedia, distributed.

2004→

Social media & YouTube, podcasts, streaming

Knowledge becomes social, viral, and on-demand.

~2006

Cloud & big data

Storage and computation become utilities.

2017→

Large language models & generative AI

Knowledge becomes inferable, not just readable.

This is where we are.

🤖 The compression revolution

What a large language model actually does, behind the chat interface, is striking. It takes a substantial fraction of recorded human text (books, articles, code, conversations…) and compresses it into a set of weights. The weights don't store the text. They store the statistical structure of how the text relates.

Every prior knowledge revolution was about storage and transmission. Cuneiform stored more than memory could. Print transmitted more than scriptoria could. The web transmitted more than print could. An LLM is the first technology in the sequence that doesn't just store or transmit, it infers. It produces new knowledge artifacts on demand.

The data center, then, becomes the new library. Not because it stores books, books have been stored fine on disk for decades, but because it's the physical site where the inference happens.

🏛️ Education: the framework always lags

Every knowledge revolution has produced a corresponding revolution in how we teach. The lag is usually one or two generations.

The Academy emerged after the alphabet made it possible to discuss texts collectively rather than memorize them individually. The medieval university emerged once manuscript culture stabilized and could support institutional libraries. The research university emerged once scientific journals had created a working scientific community. Mass schooling emerged, alongside cheap print and the industrial demand for literate, numerate workers. MOOCs and online learning emerged after the web, and have struggled, partly because they replicated the lecture format in a medium that was begging for something new.

So what does education look like when the substrate is generative inference?

Here is the unavoidable tension: most of formal education today is about transmitting and testing the explicit layer of knowledge: facts, procedures, definitions, derivations. This is precisely the layer the LLM has just commoditized. A high schooler with a frontier model has, in raw retrievable knowledge, the resources of a small university library and a patient tutor.

This does not make education obsolete. It makes the current shape of education obsolete. The parts that survive are the parts the model cannot do: tacit knowledge (the lab, the studio, the clinic), embodied practice (sports, performance, fieldwork), and phronesis (judgment under uncertainty, taste, ethics, the ability to know which question is worth asking).

There is a 2,400-year-old text that speaks directly to our moment. In Plato's Phaedrus, Socrates tells the myth of Theuth, the Egyptian god who invented writing and proudly presented it to the king. The king refused the gift. Writing will not strengthen memory, he warned, it will weaken it. Your students will appear to know much, but they will know nothing, they will be filled with the conceit of wisdom rather than wisdom itself.

Every knowledge technology since has provoked the same complaint. Print made us shallow. Calculators ruined arithmetic. Google made us forget how to find. The position on LLMs in education is this: yes, something will be lost. Some part of mental discipline, some part of the productive struggle of looking things up, some part of the muscle memory of writing your own first draft. And yes, something will be gained. The infinitely available tutor for everyone and nearly free.

The question for educators is not whether to admit this technology, but what to teach when retrieval is solved.

💼 Work: the knowledge worker, reconsidered

The same logic applies to work. Every knowledge revolution has destroyed and created categories of labor.

Roles whose value was retrieval and synthesis of explicit knowledge are being repriced in real time. It is a re-stratification of knowledge work along the tacit/explicit line. People who can move into the tacit, judgmental, embodied parts of their craft will thrive. People who were paid for the explicit, retrievable parts will compete with a machine that does it for free.

The implication for education is exactly the inverse of where most school systems currently sit. We grade what is easiest to grade, and what is easiest to grade is exactly what the model already does.

📍 Where this leaves us

The history of knowledge is a history of substrates. Bone, clay, papyrus, parchment, paper, film, magnetic tape, silicon, weights in a transformer. Each substrate has been faster, cheaper, denser, and more abstract than the last. And each substrate has produced a corresponding wave of institutions — libraries, universities, publishing houses, newsrooms, search engines, social networks.

Generative AI is the next substrate. The institutions for it have not been built yet. The educational forms for it have not been designed. The legal frameworks are partial. The job markets are still adjusting.

That is the moment we are living in. Not the end of knowledge — its largest reorganization since the printing press, and possibly since the alphabet.

If the Phoenicians made literacy learnable, and Gutenberg made it reproducible, and Berners-Lee made it linkable, the current shift makes it inferable. Knowledge that you can ask, not just read.

The new library is a data center. The new librarian is a model. The new reader is everyone, and the new writer is, increasingly, also everyone — assisted, augmented, or, depending on your view, displaced.

The question worth holding onto is the one Socrates asked the Egyptian king: will we know more, or only seem to?

The answer is not pre-decided. It depends on what we build around the technology — what schools, what professions, what laws, what habits. The substrate is here. The institutions are ours to design.
