01From framework to audit

The companion piece introduced the AI Transition Capacity Framework and applied it to 124 economies, in The AI Transition Capacity Framework. It measures how ready a workforce is for AI by combining two things: exposure, how much of the work sits in AI's path, and preparedness, whether the conditions exist to absorb it. From those two inputs it derives a Transition Capacity score and an Opportunity Score, and sorts each unit into one of four archetypes.

The framework is scale-independent. Exposure and preparedness are properties of any workforce, and a national score is just the organizations beneath it, aggregated. Point the same two measures at one company, one agency, or one team, and it lands on the same map. The difference is that an organization can act on the result. It can change its own preparedness and redesign its own roles. A country cannot do either quickly. An organization can. That is what makes the organizational version an audit rather than an observation.

02Scoring your organization

Placing an organization uses the same two inputs, estimated rather than imported from national statistics. Exposure comes from the mix of work the organization does. Preparedness comes from the conditions that determine whether it can act on AI well. The rubric below is the working version.

Exposure (E)

0.8High. Roles built on language, data, and codified knowledge. Analysts, writers, marketing, software, customer support, finance and admin, legal research.
0.5Moderate. Roles blending cognitive work with judgment, relationships, or physical presence. Managers, consultants, sales, teachers, clinicians, designers.
0.2Low. Roles dominated by manual, physical, or high-touch interpersonal work. Field technicians, trades, logistics, frontline care, hospitality.

Preparedness (P)

1Data and tooling. Access to capable AI tools and usable, accessible data.
2Skills and literacy. Practical AI competence spread across the workforce, not just a technical team.
3Change culture. Leadership backing and a track record of absorbing new ways of working.
4Governance. Usage policies, data protection, and risk controls that let people use AI safely.

Exposure is the headcount-weighted average of the role weights. Preparedness is the average of the four pillars, each rated from 0 to 4. Transition Capacity and the Opportunity Score then follow from the same two formulas used for countries, and the organization falls into one of the four archetypes.

    03From diagnosis to action: the Transition Capacity Audit

    The archetype is the diagnosis. The audit is what turns it into a plan. An archetype describes the whole organization, but AI does not arrive at the level of the organization. It arrives at the level of the task. So the audit goes one level below the score. It decomposes each exposed role into its constituent tasks and asks one question of each: automate, augment, or retain.

    • Automate. Tasks a system can take over end to end, freeing the person for other work.
    • Augment. Tasks where AI raises the speed or quality of a person's work without replacing them. This is where most durable value sits, and where the Anthropic Economic Index finds the majority of real usage today.1
    • Retain. Tasks that stay human, because of judgment, accountability, relationship, or tacit knowledge.

    This three-way split rests on the task-based approach to technological change, which analyzes labor impact at the level of tasks rather than whole jobs. In that framework, automation displaces labor from the specific tasks a machine can take over, while new and complementary tasks reinstate it elsewhere.2 Applied to AI, the same logic decomposes a role into tasks and sorts each by whether the technology substitutes for the work or complements it. That makes the classification the audit's actionable core. Each verdict routes somewhere concrete. Automate routes to tooling and process redesign. Augment routes to workflow integration and training. Retain routes to role redefinition and the reallocation of freed time.

    Run end to end, the audit follows five steps, each producing a concrete output.

    1. 1

      Map the workforce

      List roles, headcount, and the task content of each.

      Example outputFinance, 12 FTE: month-end close, invoice matching, variance commentary, board reporting.

    3. 3

    04Why training is the lever

    Look again at the four preparedness pillars. Data and tooling can be bought. Governance can be written. The pillar that moves slowest, and that most organizations score lowest on, is skills and literacy. It is also the one that decides whether any of the rest pays off.

    The audit makes this concrete. Every task marked augment depends on a person knowing how to work with the tool well. An augment verdict without training is a tool nobody uses, or uses badly, which is how most AI pilots quietly fail. The classification identifies where the value is. Training is what realizes it. This is why preparedness, not exposure, is the lever an organization actually controls, and why raising the skills pillar is usually the highest-return move available.

    It is also where the training has to be specific. Generic introductions to AI do not change how a finance team closes its books or how a clinical team handles its documentation. Workflow-led training, built around the team's real tasks, is what turns an augment classification into a changed way of working. That is the core of what TaniT Labs does.
