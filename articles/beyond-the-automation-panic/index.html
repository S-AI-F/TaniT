The AI Transition Capacity Framework

AI reshapes jobs everywhere. Who can absorb it?

A way to measure how ready any workforce is for the arrival of AI, by combining two things: how exposed its work is, and how prepared it is to absorb the technology. The framework is scale-independent. It works for a country, a region, an organization, or a team. This piece sets the foundation and applies it across 124 economies.

Saif Shabou · June 2026 · 9 min read

In brief

  • AI's impact on jobs cannot be read from exposure alone. It depends on how exposed a workforce is, how prepared it is, and the opportunity the two create together.
  • The AI Transition Capacity Framework combines these into two measures, Transition Capacity and an Opportunity Score, and works at any scale: national, subnational, organization, or team.
  • This piece builds the framework and applies it across 124 economies. A companion piece applies it inside a single organization as an audit.

01One number cannot describe AI's impact on jobs

AI's effect on work usually reaches the public as a single figure. A share of jobs exposed. A count of roles at risk. But the same level of exposure means something very different in an economy that has built digital infrastructure, skills, and governance than in one that has not.

Understanding the real impact means holding several dimensions together. How exposed an economy's jobs are to AI. How prepared the economy is to absorb the technology and deploy it well. And the opportunity that the interaction of the two creates, which is what turns exposure into productivity rather than displacement. None of these dimensions is sufficient alone. Read together, they describe situations that a single ranking flattens into one misleading number.

This matters most for the people making decisions. Strategy, investment, and policy all depend on the combination, not on exposure alone. A country that is highly exposed and well prepared needs a different playbook from one that is highly exposed and unprepared, even though both sit near the top of an exposure ranking. A framework that combines the dimensions is therefore more useful for shaping adequate strategy than any single index. That is what the AI Transition Capacity Framework sets out to do.

02Three studies, three dimensions

The framework does not start from scratch. It builds on three established bodies of research, each measuring one dimension of the problem and each authoritative in its own right.

Exposure: Felten, Raj, and Seamans. Their AI Occupational Exposure measure maps AI capabilities onto the abilities required by specific occupations, producing an exposure score for every job and, aggregated upward, for industries and geographies.1 A later extension focuses specifically on large language models.2 The consistent and counterintuitive finding: white-collar, high-wage, cognitively intensive work is more exposed than manual work, so advanced economies carry higher exposure than industrial or agrarian ones.

Preparedness: the IMF AI Preparedness Index. Introduced in the IMF's 2024 work on AI and the future of work, the AIPI scores national readiness across four determinants: digital infrastructure, human capital and labor-market policies, innovation and economic integration, and regulation and ethics.3 It answers the question exposure cannot: once AI arrives, is the country structurally able to respond.

Real-world use: the Anthropic Economic Index. Rather than forecasting, it observes. Drawing on millions of anonymized Claude conversations mapped to standardized work tasks, it measures what AI is actually being used for today, task by task, and distinguishes augmentation from automation.4

Each study answers one question. None combines all three. The framework composes the first two into a single comparative map, and returns to the third in the conclusion.

03The method

The framework works from two inputs, each normalized to a 0-to-1 scale. E is the AI Exposure Score, the share of an economy's work that is exposed to AI. P is the AI Preparedness Score, drawn from the IMF AIPI dimensions of infrastructure, human capital, innovation, and regulation. From these two inputs it derives two composite indicators.

Exposure is computed bottom up. A country's aggregate exposure is the cross-product of its workforce distribution and a standardized occupational exposure index: each occupational category's share of national employment, weighted by that category's AI exposure score, summed across all categories.

Country-level AI Exposure (E)

Ec = N∑i=1 wc,i · Si

Here wc,i is the percentage share of country c's total workforce employed in occupational category i, and Si is the standardized AI exposure score assigned to that category. The sum runs across all N occupational categories.

Two data sources feed the equation. Workforce distributions (wc,i) come from the ILO's ILOSTAT database, querying employment by occupation under the ISCO-08 classification.5 Occupational exposure scores (Si) come from the AI Occupational Exposure benchmark of Felten, Raj, and Seamans.1

AI Transition Capacity (TC)

TCi = 1 − [ Ei × (1 − Pi) ]

Transition Capacity measures a country's structural ability to integrate AI without generating catastrophic unemployment. The formula has a specific property: high exposure is not automatically a liability. It becomes one only when paired with low preparedness. A highly exposed economy with strong institutional foundations converts disruption into productivity. Without those foundations, it cannot.

AI Opportunity Score (O)

Oi = √Pi × Ei

The Opportunity Score captures the interaction between exposure and preparedness using a geometric rather than linear model. Both inputs are required to produce a meaningful score. Preparedness without exposure yields low opportunity because the economy has little cognitive surface area for AI to augment. Exposure without preparedness yields low opportunity because there is no structural capacity to capture the gain. The two conditions are necessary together.

04Four archetypes

When Transition Capacity and Opportunity Score are plotted against the two underlying inputs, preparedness and exposure, economies cluster into four structural archetypes.

The four cards below are arranged as the matrix reads: left to right is rising preparedness; top to bottom is falling exposure.

The Vulnerable Zone

High exposure · Low preparedness

Economies built on offshore BPO, basic software services, and template cognitive exports. They face direct automation pressure on their core industries but lack the safety nets or capital to reallocate displaced knowledge workers. The most structurally dangerous position.

The Advanced Frontier

High exposure · High preparedness

Knowledge-dense, service-heavy economies with mature digital infrastructure and flexible labor markets. The disruption is real and near-term, but the capacity to absorb it is highest. The probable outcome is substantial labor reallocation and compounding productivity growth, not mass unemployment.

The Left Behind

Low exposure · Low preparedness

Primary commodity, agrarian, or informal-service economies, largely insulated from immediate automation. That insulation is not a strategic asset. As frontier economies capture non-linear gains, the relative position of the Left Behind deteriorates. The threat is divergence, not disruption.

The Latent Frontier

Low exposure · High preparedness

Highly digitized economies whose current industrial mix limits immediate white-collar impact. Not facing imminent displacement, they hold the fiscal and institutional headroom to move early: infrastructure, AI service exports, regional hub positioning. The window is finite.

05Geographic distribution

The framework scores 124 economies. Before looking at any individual region, one structural fact dominates the map: exposure and preparedness are highly correlated across countries, at 0.87. Economies that have built the digital and institutional capacity to use AI are also the ones whose workforces are most exposed to it. The two move together.

This is why most countries sit on the diagonal. Of the 124, 47 fall in the Advanced Frontier and 58 in the Left Behind. Only 19 occupy the two off-diagonal archetypes, 13 in the Vulnerable Zone and 6 in the Latent Frontier. The interesting cases are rare. The dominant global pattern is a single development gradient: high exposure with high preparedness at one end, low with low at the other.

The Advanced Frontier is the developed core: all of Western Europe, North America, and advanced Asia such as Japan, Singapore, and Australia. They hold almost every top Opportunity Score. The least intuitive finding is that Eastern Europe and the Balkans have largely joined them. On these measures the preparedness gap with Western Europe has mostly closed.

The Left Behind quadrant is just as concentrated, and almost entirely Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. India sits here, not in a danger zone, which surprises readers who expect its IT and outsourcing sector to dominate. It does not, because the framework measures exposure across the whole workforce, and that workforce is still mostly agricultural and informal. A highly exposed export sector can coexist with a low national score. The sector-level risk is real; the national aggregate simply does not show it.

The off-diagonal cases are few and instructive. A small Vulnerable cluster runs through Latin America, the Caribbean, and the Western Balkans, exposed enough to feel pressure but not yet prepared to absorb it. A small Latent Frontier cluster sits in Southeast Asia, where Indonesia, the Philippines, and Thailand pair low exposure with preparedness that has crossed the threshold. Latin America is the one region split across all four archetypes, with no single story.

One reading caution. Transition Capacity runs high at both ends of the map, for opposite reasons. An advanced economy converts heavy exposure into gain, while a barely exposed economy has little to convert. The Opportunity Score separates the two, which is why the framework reports both.

The purpose of this framework is calibration, not classification. A strategy appropriate for a Latent Frontier economy fails in a Vulnerable Zone.

06Implications for strategy and policy

Generic national AI strategies ignore the difference between archetypes. The priorities below follow from each position.

Advanced Frontier

Exposed and equipped. The productivity gains arrive on their own. The distribution does not.

  • Build transition infrastructure: portable benefits, continuous education, reskilling with real uptake.
  • Invest in compute sovereignty to keep the productivity dividend partially local.

Vulnerable Zone

Exposed and underprepared, with a short window. Mostly middle-income economies with exposed service sectors.

  • Steer exposed service sectors toward human-in-the-loop AI work: validation, annotation, fine-tuning, quality assurance.
  • Build domestic digital and institutional capacity, the dimension scored lowest.
  • Move quickly. Readiness has to rise before disruption lands.

Latent Frontier

Prepared but not yet exposed. The opportunity is offensive.

  • Attract AI infrastructure with green energy zoning, clear governance, and favorable data residency law.
  • Use that investment to build local engineering capacity.
  • Move from technology consumer to regional provider.

Left Behind

Neither exposed nor prepared. AI policy is not the starting point.

  • Prioritize foundational development: electricity grid reliability, broadband coverage, digital literacy.
  • Treat AI readiness as basic economic development, not a separate AI strategy.
  • Avoid advanced AI policy before the preconditions exist.

07What the framework adds

The framework does not replace the work it draws on. It composes it. The IMF Preparedness Index says how ready a country is, the exposure measures say how much of its work sits in AI's path, and the Anthropic Economic Index says what AI is actually being used for. Each answers one question. Holding exposure and preparedness together in Transition Capacity and the Opportunity Score turns those separate signals into a single comparative map that sorts economies into archetypes. The contribution is not a new measurement, but a composition that makes the existing ones decision-useful.

Because the two inputs are properties of any workforce, the framework is not bound to the national scale. The same method places a region, an organization, or a team on the same map. At the national level it is a map for policy. At the level of a single organization it becomes an audit, and the audit becomes a plan. That is the subject of the companion piece.
